# MODULE 1 — Energy Management: General Principles, Planning & Audit

---

## 🔴 1.1 General Principles of Energy Management (3-mark Part A)

The six general principles are:

1. **Commitment from top management** — Energy management must be supported and driven from the top.
2. **Establish an energy policy** — A written policy stating energy efficiency goals.
3. **Set targets and goals** — Measurable energy reduction targets.
4. **Energy accounting and auditing** — Regular measurement and monitoring of energy use.
5. **Training and awareness** — Educate staff at all levels about energy efficiency.
6. **Continuous improvement** — Regular review and update of practices using a feedback loop.

---

## 🔴 1.2 Objectives / Significance of Energy Management (3-mark)

1. To reduce energy costs in industrial and commercial facilities.
2. To identify wasteful energy practices and eliminate them.
3. To improve the efficiency of energy-using equipment.
4. To reduce dependence on fossil fuels.
5. To comply with energy conservation regulations and policies.
6. To reduce greenhouse gas emissions and environmental impact.

---

## 🔴 1.3 Phases of Energy Management Planning (8–10 marks)

Energy management planning is carried out in **three phases**:

### Phase 1: Recognition Phase
- Identify the need and commitment for energy management.
- Appoint an energy manager.
- Define the scope of the programme.
- Obtain management support and allocate budget.

### Phase 2: Audit and Analysis Phase
This is the most detailed phase. Information collected includes:
- **Historical energy data** — utility bills, fuel purchase records for the past 2–3 years.
- **Building/plant inventory** — floor area, equipment list, production data.
- **Energy consuming systems** — lighting, HVAC, motors, furnaces, boilers.
- **Operating patterns** — shifts, seasonal variations, occupancy schedules.
- **Baseline energy consumption** — energy per unit output (specific energy consumption).
- **Identification of energy waste areas** — through walkthrough and detailed audit.

### Phase 3: Implementation Phase
- Prioritize energy conservation measures (ECMs) based on cost-benefit analysis.
- Implement no-cost and low-cost measures immediately.
- Prepare detailed project proposals for capital-intensive measures.
- Monitor and verify savings after implementation.
- Report results to management.

> For a technical education building: collect data on lighting load (kWh/month), HVAC usage, computer lab consumption, canteen load, annual utility bills, occupancy hours per day/week.

## 🔴 1.4 Energy Audit — Definition, Need, Types, Steps (8–10 marks)

### Definition
An energy audit is a **systematic examination of energy flows** in a facility to identify where energy is being used, how efficiently it is used, and where it can be saved.

### Need / Why conduct an energy audit
1. Rising energy costs eat into profit margins.
2. To identify specific areas of energy waste.
3. To develop a prioritized list of energy conservation opportunities (ECOs).
4. Mandatory under the Energy Conservation Act 2001 for designated consumers.
5. To reduce carbon footprint and environmental impact.

### Types of Energy Audit

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Preliminary / Walk-through Audit** | Quick visual inspection; identifies obvious wastage; low cost; gives rough estimate of savings potential. |
| **Detailed / Diagnostic Audit** | In-depth analysis with measurements; uses instruments; quantifies energy flows; develops investment-grade proposals. |
| **Investment-grade Audit** | Highest level; full techno-economic analysis; used for securing financing. |

### Steps in a Detailed Energy Audit

1. **Pre-audit preparation** — Collect historical utility bills, plant layout, equipment details.
2. **Walkthrough survey** — Visual inspection of all energy systems.
3. **Measurement and monitoring** — Use instruments (energy meters, lux meters, flue gas analysers, etc.) to measure energy consumption.
4. **Data analysis** — Calculate specific energy consumption, identify deviations from benchmarks.
5. **Identification of ECOs** — List all energy conservation opportunities.
6. **Financial analysis** — Payback period, NPV, IRR for each ECO.
7. **Report preparation** — Present findings and recommendations.
8. **Implementation and follow-up** — Carry out the recommended measures and verify savings.

## 🔴 1.6 Energy Audit Report (4–7 marks)

The energy audit report is the final deliverable of an energy audit. It documents the findings and recommendations.

**General format / contents of an energy audit report:**

1. **Executive Summary** — Brief overview of findings and top recommendations.
2. **Introduction** — Background, scope, and objectives of the audit.
3. **Description of the facility** — Plant/building details, production process, utility systems.
4. **Energy consumption analysis** — Historical energy data, specific energy consumption, benchmarking.
5. **Energy conservation opportunities (ECOs)** — Detailed description of each opportunity found.
6. **Financial analysis** — Cost of implementation, annual savings, payback period for each ECO.
7. **Prioritized action plan** — List of ECOs ranked by ease of implementation and payback.
8. **Appendices** — Raw data, equipment specifications, measurement records.

## 🔴 1.7 Power Quality Audit (7–10 marks)

### Why power quality audit is needed
- Poor power quality causes equipment failure, overheating, increased losses, and reduced efficiency.
- It affects the accuracy of energy consumption measurements.
- Helps identify causes of high kVAR demand and penalties on electricity bills.
- Identifies harmonic distortion which increases transformer and motor losses.

### Power Quality Parameters

| Parameter | Definition |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Voltage sag/swell** | Temporary decrease/increase in RMS voltage (0.5–30 cycles). |
| **Harmonics (THD)** | Distortion of sinusoidal waveform due to non-linear loads (VFDs, computers). |
| **Power Factor** | Ratio of active power (kW) to apparent power (kVA). Low PF = poor quality. |
| **Flicker** | Rapid fluctuations in voltage causing light flicker; common near arc furnaces. |
| **Voltage unbalance** | Difference in magnitude among three phases; causes motor overheating. |
| **Transients / Spikes** | Short-duration high-voltage events caused by switching or lightning. |
| **Frequency deviation** | Deviation from 50 Hz; affects synchronous and induction motor speeds. |

**How it is carried out:** Using a Power Quality Analyser connected at the main distribution board, data is recorded over 24–48 hours to capture all operating conditions. Results are analysed and corrective measures (filters, capacitor banks, UPS) are recommended.

**Does it improve energy efficiency?** Yes — by correcting power factor, reducing harmonics (which reduce copper and iron losses in transformers/motors), and eliminating unnecessary reactive power.

## 🟡 1.8 Energy Conservation Building Code (ECBC) (4–7 marks)

The ECBC is a code issued by the **Bureau of Energy Efficiency (BEE)** under the Ministry of Power, Government of India, under the Energy Conservation Act 2001.

**Scope:** Applies to new commercial buildings with a connected load ≥ 500 kW or contract demand ≥ 600 kVA.

**Key aspects covered:**
1. **Building envelope** — Wall, roof, and glazing U-values (insulation requirements) to reduce heat gain/loss.
2. **Lighting** — Maximum Lighting Power Density (LPD) in W/m² for each space type; use of daylighting.
3. **HVAC systems** — Minimum COP/EER standards for chillers and air conditioners.
4. **Electrical systems** — Power factor requirements, transformer efficiency standards.
5. **Renewable energy** — Encouragement of solar water heating and rooftop solar.

**ECBC Star Rating:** Buildings can be rated from ECBC Compliant to ECBC+ and Super ECBC based on the percentage of energy saving over baseline.

## 🟡 1.9 Building Management System (BMS) (6 marks)

A BMS (also called Building Automation System) is a **computer-based control system** that monitors and controls a building's mechanical and electrical systems.

**Systems controlled by BMS:**
- HVAC (heating, ventilation, air conditioning)
- Lighting
- Fire and life safety systems
- Security and access control
- Lifts and escalators
- Energy metering and monitoring

**How BMS saves energy:**
1. **Automatic scheduling** — Turns off HVAC and lights when rooms are unoccupied.
2. **Optimum start/stop** — Calculates the latest possible start time to reach comfort conditions.
3. **Demand control ventilation** — Adjusts fresh air intake based on CO₂ sensors.
4. **Central monitoring** — Real-time energy dashboards; alerts for abnormal consumption.
5. **Integration** — Coordinates multiple systems for overall efficiency (e.g., increase chiller setpoint when outside temperature is low).

## 🔴 2.1 Cascade Efficiency in Electrical Systems (6–7 marks)

**Definition:** Cascade efficiency is the **overall efficiency of a system** that consists of multiple stages connected in series, calculated as the product of the efficiencies of each individual stage.

$$\eta_{cascade} = \eta_1 \times \eta_2 \times \eta_3 \times \ldots \times \eta_n$$

**Example — Electricity transmission system:**

| Stage | Typical Efficiency |
|-------|------------------|
| Generation (thermal power plant) | ~35–38% |
| Step-up transformer (generator → transmission) | ~99% |
| Transmission line | ~97% |
| Step-down transformer (transmission → distribution) | ~98.5% |
| Distribution transformer | ~97% |
| Motor (end use) | ~90% |
| **Overall (Generation to shaft output)** | ~35% × 0.99 × 0.97 × 0.985 × 0.97 × 0.90 ≈ **~28%** |

**How to improve cascade efficiency:**
1. Use high-efficiency transformers (amorphous core or CRGO core).
2. Use energy-efficient motors (IE3/IE4 class).
3. Minimize transmission and distribution losses by maintaining high power factor.
4. Use Variable Frequency Drives (VFDs) for variable loads.
5. Proper conductor sizing to reduce I²R losses.
6. Replace old equipment with modern high-efficiency equivalents.

## 🔴 2.2 Energy Management in Lighting (8 marks)

### Modern Energy Efficient Light Sources

| Source | Efficacy (lm/W) | Life (hours) |
|--------|----------------|-------------|
| Incandescent lamp | 8–15 | 1,000 |
| Fluorescent tube (T8) | 60–80 | 10,000–15,000 |
| CFL | 50–70 | 8,000–10,000 |
| **LED** | **80–150** | **25,000–50,000** |
| Metal Halide | 80–100 | 10,000–20,000 |
| High Pressure Sodium | 80–150 | 12,000–24,000 |

**Efficacy** = Luminous flux (lumens) / Power input (watts)

### Six Methods / Energy Management Opportunities in Lighting

1. **Replace old lamps with LEDs** — LEDs have 5–10× higher efficacy than incandescent; 25,000–50,000 hour life.
2. **Use of occupancy/motion sensors (PIR sensors)** — Automatically switch off lights when a room is unoccupied.
3. **Daylighting / natural light integration** — Use skylights, light shelves, and automatic dimming controls to reduce artificial lighting during daytime.
4. **Task lighting** — Provide focused lighting only where needed instead of uniform high-level lighting throughout.
5. **Use of electronic ballasts** — Replace magnetic ballasts in fluorescent fittings with electronic ballasts (reduce energy by 25%, eliminate flicker).
6. **Lighting automation / DALI/BMS control** — Centrally schedule and dim lights based on time of day, occupancy, and daylight availability.
7. **Delamping** — Remove excess lamps in over-lit areas without compromising lux levels.
8. **Regular cleaning** — Clean lamps and fixtures to prevent lumen depreciation (dirty fixtures can lose 20–30% output).

## 🔴 2.3 Energy Management in Electric Motors (8–10 marks)

### Features of Energy Efficient Motors (EEM)
1. Reduced stator copper losses (larger conductor cross-section).
2. Reduced rotor copper losses (more aluminium or copper in rotor bars).
3. Reduced core (iron) losses using premium grade CRGO steel laminations.
4. Reduced friction and windage losses (better bearings, improved fan design).
5. Reduced stray load losses.
6. Operate at higher power factor and efficiency across the load range.
7. Classified as IE2 (high efficiency), IE3 (premium), IE4 (super premium) per IEC 60034-30.

### Methods / Techniques to Improve Energy Efficiency of Motors

1. **Replace standard motors with EEMs** — IE3/IE4 motors have 2–5% higher efficiency.
2. **Load matching (Right-sizing)** — Motors running below 50% load have poor efficiency and PF. Replace oversized motors with properly sized ones.
3. **Use of Variable Frequency Drives (VFDs)** — For variable torque loads (fans, pumps), reducing speed by 20% reduces power by ~50% (affinity laws: P ∝ N³).
4. **Power factor correction** — Install capacitors at motor terminals to improve PF and reduce kVA demand.
5. **Rewinding practices** — Poorly rewound motors lose 1–2% efficiency. Use quality rewinding or replace with new EEM.
6. **Proper motor maintenance** — Regular lubrication, alignment, belt tension checks.
7. **Avoid frequent starts/stops** — Use soft starters to reduce inrush current and mechanical stress.
8. **Shut down idling motors** — Motors running unloaded consume 25–35% of full-load power.

### Load Matching and Selection
- **Constant torque loads** (conveyors, compressors): Select motor rated for continuous duty at that torque.
- **Variable torque loads** (fans, pumps): Use VFD; motor can be selected slightly larger but VFD saves energy.
- Operating a motor below 40% of rated load causes significant efficiency and PF drop.
- **Rule of thumb:** Select motor so that it operates between 75–100% of rated load for best efficiency.

### Types of Motor Losses

| Loss Type | Cause |
|-----------|-------|
| Stator copper loss (I²R) | Current flowing through stator resistance |
| Rotor copper loss (I²R) | Current flowing through rotor bars |
| Core (iron) loss | Hysteresis and eddy currents in stator core; nearly constant with load |
| Friction and windage loss | Bearings, cooling fan; nearly constant |
| Stray load loss | Leakage flux, harmonics; increases with load |

## 🔴 2.4 Transformer Efficiency and Design Measures (4–8 marks)

### Types of Transformer Losses

| Loss | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Core (iron/no-load) loss** | Hysteresis + eddy current loss in the core; constant regardless of load |
| **Copper (load) loss** | I²R loss in windings; proportional to (load)² |

### Efficiency Formula
$$\eta = \frac{P_{out}}{P_{out} + P_{core} + P_{copper}} \times 100\%$$

**Maximum efficiency** occurs when: Core loss = Copper loss

### Present Maximum Efficiency Standards (BEE India)
- Distribution transformers (up to 200 kVA): minimum efficiency ~98.5–99%
- Power transformers (>1 MVA): minimum ~99.3%
- "Star-1" and "Star-2" label transformers under BEE have higher efficiency standards.

### Design Measures to Increase Efficiency

1. **Use amorphous metal core** — Amorphous steel has ~70% lower core loss than CRGO; ideal for distribution transformers.
2. **Use CRGO (Cold Rolled Grain Oriented) silicon steel** — Lower hysteresis loss than HRGOS.
3. **Optimum flux density** — Operate below saturation to minimize core loss.
4. **Increase conductor cross-section** — Reduces copper loss (I²R).
5. **Use copper windings instead of aluminium** — Lower resistivity = lower copper loss.
6. **Improve insulation quality** — Prevents partial discharge losses.
7. **Load balancing** — Avoid running transformers at very low load (low efficiency region); consolidate loads.
8. **Loss evaluation approach** — Specify transformers on Total Owning Cost (TOC = capital cost + capitalised cost of losses) rather than just purchase price.

## 🔴 3.1 Demand Side Management (DSM) — Intro, Benefits, Techniques (7 marks)

### Introduction
DSM refers to the **planning and implementation of activities by utilities or governments** to influence the amount and timing of electricity use by consumers, so that the shape of the load curve is modified to achieve desired objectives.

### Benefits of DSM
1. Reduces peak demand → defers need for new generation capacity investment.
2. Reduces electricity bills for consumers.
3. Improves load factor of the utility.
4. Reduces transmission and distribution losses.
5. Reduces environmental impact (lower fuel burning at peak).
6. Enhances energy security.

### Different Techniques of DSM

1. **Peak Clipping** — Direct reduction of peak demand by utilities (e.g., remotely switching off water heaters, ACs during peak).
2. **Valley Filling** — Adding load during off-peak hours (e.g., water heating, EV charging at night) to improve load factor.
3. **Load Shifting** — Moving loads from peak to off-peak periods (e.g., laundry, industrial batch processes shifted to night).
4. **Strategic Conservation** — Overall reduction of energy consumption without shifting time; achieved by energy efficiency measures.
5. **Strategic Load Growth** — Increasing electricity sales in targeted market segments (e.g., promoting heat pumps over gas heating).
6. **Flexible Load Shape** — Interruptible/curtailable service contracts; utility can curtail load with customer consent.

### DSM and Environment
- Avoids construction of peaking power plants (which are often inefficient diesel/gas turbines).
- Reduces CO₂, NOx, SOx emissions.
- Delays the need for new transmission lines and substations.

---

## 🔴 3.2 Time-of-Day (ToD) Pricing (3–7 marks)

**Definition:** Time-of-Day (ToD) pricing (also called Time-of-Use or TOU tariff) is an electricity tariff structure where the **price per kWh varies depending on the time of day**.

- **Peak hours** (e.g., 6 PM–10 PM): Higher tariff rate.
- **Off-peak hours** (e.g., 10 PM–6 AM): Lower tariff rate.
- **Shoulder hours**: Intermediate rate.

**Purpose:**
- Incentivises consumers to shift loads away from peak hours.
- Signals the true cost of electricity at different times.
- Reduces peak demand on the grid.

**Multi-utility power exchange model:** Multiple utilities share generation resources, buying and selling power to each other during different periods. When utility A has a valley, it sells cheap power to utility B which is at peak, and vice versa. This reduces the need for each utility to maintain large peaking plants.

## 🔴 3.3 Peak Demand Control Methods (7–8 marks)

### Importance
- Peak demand charges constitute a significant portion of industrial electricity bills (100–1000 Rs/kVA).
- Utilities size their infrastructure (transformers, cables, switchgear) for peak demand.
- Reducing peak demand directly reduces the maximum demand charge and infrastructure costs.

### Peak Demand Control Methodologies

1. **Load Curve Generation**
   - Plot a daily load curve (kVA vs time of day).
   - Identify the peak period.
   - All control strategies target this peak period.

2. **Rescheduling of Loads**
   - Shift high-demand processes/equipment to off-peak hours.
   - Example: Run large compressors, furnaces, or batch processes at night.
   - Prepare an operation flow chart; reschedule to improve load factor.

3. **Storage of Products/Process Utilities**
   - Build up inventory during off-peak hours so production can slow down during peak.
   - Example: **Ice bank system** in dairy — make ice at night, use it for cooling during the day.
   - Chilled water storage tanks in HVAC.

4. **Shedding of Non-Essential Loads**
   - Install a **demand controller** that monitors real-time demand.
   - When demand approaches the preset limit, automatically shed non-essential loads (e.g., non-critical lighting, water heaters, non-critical air conditioners).
   - Sophisticated microprocessor systems can: predict demand, give audio/visual alarm, auto-shed and auto-restore loads.

5. **Operation of DG Sets**
   - Connect diesel generators during peak demand periods to supplement grid supply.
   - Reduces kVA drawn from utility → reduces maximum demand charge.

6. **Reactive Power Compensation**
   - Install capacitor banks to improve power factor.
   - High PF → lower kVA for same kW → lower maximum demand charge.
   - Automatic Power Factor Control (APFC) panels switch capacitors in/out to maintain target PF.

## 🔴 3.4 Power Factor Improvement (7 marks)

### Benefits of Power Factor Improvement
1. Reduction in kVA demand → reduction in maximum demand charges.
2. Reduction in I²R losses in cables and transformers (for same kW, current is lower at higher PF).
3. Reduction in voltage drop across cables.
4. Increased capacity of existing cables and transformers (can carry more kW load).
5. Reduction in electricity bill (most industrial tariffs penalise low PF).

### Most Economical Power Factor

The annual cost = kVA tariff × (kW/cos φ) + annual cost of capacitors per kVAR × kW(tan φ₁ - tan φ)

Let the cost per kVAR of capacitor = C_c per annum
Let the tariff = T Rs per kVA per annum

**At most economical PF:**
$$\sin\phi_{opt} = \frac{C_c}{T}$$
$$\cos\phi_{opt} = \sqrt{1 - \sin^2\phi_{opt}}$$

### Calculation of kVAR required

From power triangle:
$$\text{Leading kVAR required} = kW(\tan\phi_1 - \tan\phi_2)$$

Where φ₁ = initial PF angle, φ₂ = improved PF angle.

### Numerical Example (from PYQ — 800 kW, 0.8 pf → 0.9 pf):

**Given:**
- P = 800 kW, initial pf cos φ₁ = 0.8, improved pf cos φ₂ = 0.9
- Working hours = 3000 hr/year
- Tariff = Rs 100/kVA + Rs 0.20/kWh
- Capacitor cost = Rs 60/kVAR, interest & depreciation = 10%/year

**Step 1: Initial kVA = 800/0.8 = 1000 kVA**
**Step 2: New kVA = 800/0.9 = 888.9 kVA**
**Step 3: Reduction in kVA = 111.1 kVA**
**Step 4: Annual saving on kVA charge = 111.1 × 100 = Rs 11,111**
**Step 5: kVAR required = 800(tan37° - tan26°) = 800(0.75 - 0.484) = 800 × 0.266 = 212.7 kVAR**
(cos⁻¹0.8 = 36.87°, tan = 0.75; cos⁻¹0.9 = 25.84°, tan = 0.484)
**Step 6: Annual cost of capacitors = 212.7 × 60 × 0.10 = Rs 1276**
**Step 7: Net annual saving = 11,111 − 1,276 = Rs 9,835**

## 🔴 3.5 Ancillary Services (6–7 marks)

### Definition
Ancillary services are **support services provided to a power system** to maintain reliability, security, and power quality of the grid, in addition to the main energy supply.

### Types of Ancillary Services

1. **Frequency Regulation (Regulation Service)**
   - Maintains grid frequency at 50 Hz.
   - Generators that can rapidly increase or decrease output in response to automatic generation control (AGC) signals.

2. **Load Following**
   - Generators that can follow slower changes in load over minutes to hours.
   - Slower than frequency regulation but handles larger deviations.

3. **Spinning Reserve**
   - Generation capacity that is already synchronised to the grid but operating below full output.
   - Can ramp up within 10 minutes to replace a tripped generator.

4. **Non-Spinning (Supplemental) Reserve**
   - Generation capacity that is not currently online but can be started and synchronised within 10–30 minutes.

5. **Reactive Power / Voltage Support**
   - Generators, capacitor banks, and STATCOMs that supply/absorb reactive power to maintain voltage profile.

6. **Black Start Capability**
   - Ability of a generator to start without external power supply, used to restore the grid after a blackout.

7. **Energy Imbalance Service**
   - Corrects real-time imbalances between scheduled and actual generation/load.

## 🔴 4.1 Energy Conservation in Boilers (7 marks)

### Working Principle of a Boiler (brief)
A boiler converts chemical energy of fuel into heat energy, which is transferred to water/steam. Efficiency is typically 70–85% for industrial boilers.

### Energy Conservation Opportunities in Boilers

1. **Flue gas heat recovery** — Use an economiser (preheats feed water) or air preheater (preheats combustion air) to recover heat from flue gases. Every 6°C drop in flue gas temperature ≈ 1% improvement in efficiency.

2. **Excess air control** — Minimise excess air while ensuring complete combustion. Every 1% reduction in excess air ≈ 0.6% improvement in efficiency. Use O₂ analyser for control.

3. **Boiler blowdown heat recovery** — Recover heat from blowdown water using a flash vessel (generates low pressure steam) and heat exchanger (preheats make-up water).

4. **Steam trap maintenance** — Faulty steam traps waste steam. Regular testing and replacement can save 10–15% of steam generation.

5. **Insulation of steam lines and boiler surfaces** — Reduces radiation and convection losses.

6. **Feedwater treatment** — Prevents scale formation on heat transfer surfaces. 1 mm of scale increases fuel consumption by ~2%.

7. **Automatic combustion control** — Optimises fuel/air ratio automatically based on load.

8. **Reduce steam pressure** — Operate at the minimum required pressure; lower pressure = less heat loss from distribution lines.

9. **Flash steam recovery** — High-pressure condensate flashes to lower pressure steam when its pressure is reduced; this flash steam can be used in low-pressure applications.

### Boiler Blowdown (asked separately — 6 marks)

**Need:** During boiler operation, dissolved solids in feedwater concentrate. If not removed, they cause scale formation and foaming (carryover). Blowdown removes this concentrated water to maintain TDS (Total Dissolved Solids) within limits.

**Two sources of feedwater:** (1) Make-up water (from external source), (2) Condensate return.

**Types of Blowdown:**
1. **Bottom blowdown (intermittent)** — Opens drain at the bottom; removes sludge and suspended solids. Done periodically (daily/weekly).
2. **Surface / Continuous blowdown** — Removes concentrated water from the water surface level continuously; controls TDS. The blown-down water can be sent to a heat recovery system.

## 🔴 4.2 Energy Savings in Steam Distribution (7 marks)

### Properties of Steam
- Wet steam, dry saturated steam, superheated steam.
- Steam carries latent heat; higher pressure = higher temperature = more heat content per kg.

### Energy Saving Opportunities in Steam Distribution

1. **Steam trap maintenance and replacement** — Failed-open traps waste live steam; failed-closed traps cause waterlogging. Survey all traps quarterly using acoustic/temperature methods.

2. **Proper insulation of steam pipes** — Reduces heat loss from pipe surfaces. Use mineral wool/calcium silicate insulation. Repair damaged insulation promptly.

3. **Eliminate leaks** — Steam leaks from flanges, valves, and glands are visible and audible. A 3 mm hole at 7 bar pressure wastes ~30 kg/hr of steam.

4. **Condensate recovery and return** — Condensate is hot, treated, and contains valuable heat energy. Returning condensate to the boiler reduces make-up water, fuel consumption, and chemical treatment costs.

5. **Flash steam utilisation** — When high-pressure condensate is flashed to lower pressure, the flash steam generated should be used in low-pressure applications rather than vented.

6. **Correct pipe sizing** — Oversized pipes have higher surface area → more heat loss. Undersized pipes cause excessive pressure drop.

7. **Pressure reduction** — Supply steam at only the pressure needed for each process; use pressure reducing valves (PRVs).

8. **Elimination of steam leaks and condensate lines** — Using ultrasonic detectors to find leaks.

## 🔴 4.3 Energy Conservation in Furnaces (7 marks)

### General Fuel Economy Measures / Energy Conservation Opportunities

1. **Recuperators and regenerators** — Preheat combustion air using flue gases. Preheating air by 100°C saves ~5–6% fuel. Regenerative burners can recover 60–70% of flue gas heat.

2. **Optimum excess air** — Minimise excess air for complete combustion. Monitor using O₂/CO analysers.

3. **Proper furnace insulation** — Use ceramic fibre lining for intermittent/batch furnaces (faster heat-up, lower heat storage loss). Repair cracks and holes.

4. **Minimise furnace opening** — Each door opening causes significant heat loss (radiation and convection). Minimise frequency and duration.

5. **Correct furnace temperature control** — Use thermocouple-based automatic temperature controllers; avoid overshooting setpoint.

6. **Load optimisation** — Maximise furnace loading per batch to improve energy per unit product.

7. **Waste heat recovery** — Use flue gases to preheat charge material (billets, ingots) using waste heat chambers.

8. **Furnace pressure control** — Maintain slight positive pressure to prevent cold air infiltration (which wastes heat on unnecessary heating of cold air).

## 🔴 4.4 Cogeneration (7–8 marks)

### Definition
Cogeneration (Combined Heat and Power — CHP) is the **simultaneous generation of electrical (or mechanical) energy and useful heat from a single fuel source**. It achieves overall fuel utilisation of 70–90% vs ~35% for a conventional power plant.

### Advantages
1. Higher overall fuel efficiency.
2. Reduced energy costs.
3. Reduced greenhouse gas emissions.
4. On-site generation → less dependence on grid; better reliability.

### Types of Cogeneration Systems

**1. Topping Cycle (most common)**
- Fuel is first used to generate electricity/mechanical power; waste heat is then used for process heating.
- Subtypes:
  - **Gas turbine topping cycle** — Gas turbine drives generator; exhaust gas (500–550°C) used in HRSG to produce steam for process.
  - **Steam turbine topping cycle** — High-pressure steam drives turbine (generates electricity); exhaust/extracted steam used for process heating.
  - **Reciprocating engine topping cycle** — IC engine drives generator; jacket cooling water + exhaust gas heat used for process.

**2. Bottoming Cycle**
- Fuel is first used for high-temperature industrial process (e.g., furnace); waste heat from the process is then used to generate electricity using a waste heat recovery boiler + steam turbine.
- Less common; used in cement, glass, and steel industries.

**3. Combined Cycle Power Generation**
- Gas turbine generates electricity; exhaust gas goes to HRSG to produce steam; steam turbine generates additional electricity.
- Overall efficiency ~55–60%.
- Used in large power plants.

## 🔴 4.5 HVAC — COP and Energy Saving Opportunities (3–7 marks)

### Coefficient of Performance (COP)
$$COP_{refrigerator} = \frac{\text{Refrigerating effect (kW)}}{\text{Work input (kW)}} = \frac{Q_L}{W}$$

$$COP_{heat pump} = \frac{Q_H}{W} = COP_{refrigerator} + 1$$

COP ≠ Efficiency (efficiency is always ≤ 1; COP can be > 1 for refrigerators and heat pumps).

**How HVAC capacity is measured:** In Ton of Refrigeration (TR) — 1 TR = 3.517 kW of cooling.

### Energy Saving Opportunities in HVAC

1. **Variable speed drives on chiller pumps and AHU fans** — Reduce speed during part-load conditions.
2. **Increase chiller setpoint temperature** — Every 1°C increase in chilled water supply temperature improves COP by ~2–3%.
3. **Condenser water temperature reduction** — Better cooling tower performance → lower condenser pressure → higher COP.
4. **Regular cleaning of heat exchangers** — Scale/fouling on condenser/evaporator reduces heat transfer efficiency.
5. **Building envelope improvement** — Better insulation, double-glazed windows reduce cooling load.
6. **Night setback/setpoint control** — Raise setpoint at night or when unoccupied.
7. **Free cooling (economiser mode)** — Use outdoor air directly when ambient temperature is suitable.
8. **Energy recovery from exhaust air** — Use heat exchangers to pre-condition incoming fresh air.

---

## 🔴 4.6 Waste Heat Recovery Systems (7 marks)

### Definition
Waste heat recovery is the capture of heat that would otherwise be lost to the atmosphere from industrial processes and using it productively.

### Types of Waste Heat Recovery Systems

**1. Recuperators**
- A **direct heat exchanger** between flue gas and incoming cold air/fluid.
- Countercurrent flow gives best heat transfer.
- Application: Preheat combustion air in furnaces/boilers.
- Simple, low maintenance.

**2. Regenerators**
- Use a **heat storage matrix** (ceramic or metal checkerwork) that absorbs heat from hot gas and releases it to cold incoming gas alternately.
- Two chambers used alternately (valve switching).
- Application: Glass melting furnaces, open hearth furnaces.
- Can recover 60–70% of waste heat.

**3. Heat Pipe Heat Exchanger**
- Uses **sealed pipes containing a working fluid** that evaporates at the hot end and condenses at the cold end, transferring heat with minimal temperature difference.
- No moving parts; highly reliable.
- Application: Air-to-air heat recovery in HVAC, process gas heat recovery.

**4. Waste Heat Boilers (Heat Recovery Steam Generators — HRSG)**
- Hot flue gases pass through a boiler to generate steam or hot water.
- Application: After gas turbines, cement kilns, glass furnaces.

### Energy Saving Opportunities in WHR
1. Preheat combustion air (saves 20–30% fuel in furnaces).
2. Preheat process feed materials.
3. Generate steam for process use or electricity (combined cycle / bottoming cycle).
4. Space heating and hot water production.

## 🔴 5.1 Simple Payback Period (SPP) (3–7 marks)

$$\text{Payback Period} = \frac{\text{Capital Investment (Rs.)}}{\text{Net Annual Savings (Rs./year)}}$$

**Net Annual Savings = Annual Energy Savings − Annual Operating & Maintenance Costs**

### Advantages
1. Simple to calculate and understand.
2. Gives a quick first estimate of project viability.
3. Useful for comparing projects with similar risk profiles.

### Disadvantages
1. Ignores time value of money.
2. Ignores cash flows after the payback period.
3. Does not account for project life or salvage value.
4. Not suitable for comparing projects with very different lives.

**Numerical example (from PYQ):**
- Annual energy saving = Rs 4,86,000
- Annual O&M cost = Rs 42,000
- Capital cost = Rs 22,20,000
- Net annual saving = 4,86,000 − 42,000 = Rs 4,44,000
- **Payback period = 22,20,000 / 4,44,000 = 5 years**

---

## 🔴 5.2 Net Present Value (NPV) Method (7 marks)

### Concept — Time Value of Money
Money available today is worth more than the same amount in the future due to its earning potential. The **present value (PV)** of a future cash flow is:
$$PV = \frac{F}{(1+i)^n}$$
Where F = future cash flow, i = discount rate, n = year.

### NPV Formula
$$NPV = \sum_{n=1}^{N} \frac{S_n}{(1+i)^n} - C_0$$

Where S_n = saving in year n, C₀ = initial investment, i = discount rate.

**Decision rule:** If NPV > 0, the project is economically viable. Higher NPV = better project.

### Advantages of NPV
1. Accounts for time value of money.
2. Considers all cash flows over the project life.
3. Gives absolute value of wealth creation.

### Disadvantages / Drawbacks
1. Requires estimation of discount rate (subjective).
2. Difficult to compare projects of different sizes.
3. Assumes cash flows are reinvested at the discount rate.

### Numerical (from PYQ — Investment Rs 12 lakh, savings Rs 1.5L, 2L, 3L at 10%):
$$NPV = \frac{1,50,000}{1.1^1} + \frac{2,00,000}{1.1^2} + \frac{3,00,000}{1.1^3} - 12,00,000$$
$$= 1,36,364 + 1,65,289 + 2,25,394 - 12,00,000$$
$$= 5,27,047 - 12,00,000 = -6,72,953$$
Since NPV < 0, the project is **not viable**.

### Numerical (from PYQ — Investment Rs 10 lakh, 5 years):

| Year | Cash Flow | PV Factor (10%) | Present Value |
|------|----------|----------------|--------------|
| 1 | 2,00,000 | 0.909 | 1,81,800 |
| 2 | 2,00,000 | 0.826 | 1,65,200 |
| 3 | 3,00,000 | 0.751 | 2,25,300 |
| 4 | 3,00,000 | 0.683 | 2,04,900 |
| 5 | 3,50,000 | 0.621 | 2,17,350 |
| **Total PV** | | | **9,94,550** |

NPV = 9,94,550 − 10,00,000 = **−Rs 5,450** (marginally negative; project barely not viable)

## 🔴 5.3 Life Cycle Costing (LCC) Approach (7–10 marks)

### Definition
LCC is a method that evaluates the **total cost of ownership** of a project over its entire life, including initial cost, operating costs, maintenance costs, and salvage value.

$$LCC = C_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{N} \frac{C_{op,n}}{(1+i)^n} - \frac{S_N}{(1+i)^N}$$

Where C₀ = initial capital cost, C_op = annual operating cost, S_N = salvage value, N = life, i = discount rate.

### Steps in LCC Approach for Energy Project Selection

1. **Define the alternatives** — Identify all options (e.g., keep old motor vs buy EEM).
2. **Determine the analysis period** — Usually the life of the equipment.
3. **Estimate all cost elements:**
   - Initial capital cost (purchase, installation)
   - Annual energy cost (energy consumed × unit price)
   - Annual maintenance cost
   - Salvage value at end of life
4. **Select discount rate** — Based on cost of capital or minimum acceptable rate of return (MARR).
5. **Calculate present worth of all future costs** using PV factors.
6. **Calculate Total LCC** for each alternative.
7. **Select the alternative with the lowest LCC.**

### Advantages of LCC
1. Considers total cost over life, not just first cost.
2. Accounts for time value of money.
3. Allows fair comparison of energy-efficient (higher first cost, lower running cost) vs conventional equipment.
4. Helps justify premium-priced energy-efficient products.

## 🔴 5.4 Internal Rate of Return (IRR) (7–8 marks)

### Definition
IRR is the **discount rate at which the NPV of a project equals zero**. It is the rate of return earned by the project.

$$0 = \sum_{n=1}^{N} \frac{S_n}{(1+IRR)^n} - C_0$$

### Decision Rule
- If IRR > MARR (Minimum Acceptable Rate of Return / cost of capital) → Accept the project.
- If IRR < MARR → Reject the project.
- For comparing two projects: choose the one with higher IRR.

### Calculation Method
IRR is found by **trial and interpolation:**

1. Calculate NPV at a lower discount rate (i₁) → positive NPV₁.
2. Calculate NPV at a higher discount rate (i₂) → negative NPV₂.
3. Interpolate:
$$IRR = i_1 + \frac{NPV_1}{NPV_1 - NPV_2} \times (i_2 - i_1)$$

### Numerical (from PYQ — Cost Rs 5 lakh, life 10 years, saving Rs 1.5 lakh/year):
- At i = 27%: NPV ≈ 0 (check using annuity factor: PV factor for annuity at 27%, 10 years = 3.33; 1,50,000 × 3.33 = 4,99,500 ≈ 5,00,000)
- **IRR ≈ 27%**

## 🟡 5.5 Average Rate of Return (ARR) (3–4 marks)

$$ARR = \frac{\text{Average annual net savings}}{\text{Average investment}} \times 100\%$$

Average investment = (Initial investment + Salvage value) / 2

### Advantages: Simple; easy to compare with target rate of return.
### Disadvantages: Ignores time value of money; uses accounting profits, not cash flows.

**Numerical (from PYQ — Investment Rs 1,00,000, life 5 years, inflows Rs 20,000/20,000/10,000/10,000/30,000, salvage Rs 25,000):**
- Total inflows = 20,000 + 20,000 + 10,000 + 10,000 + 30,000 = 90,000
- Average annual saving = 90,000 / 5 = 18,000
- Average investment = (1,00,000 + 25,000) / 2 = 62,500
- **ARR = (18,000 / 62,500) × 100 = 28.8%**

## 🟡 5.6 Computer Aided Energy Management System (EMS) (3–7 marks)

A **Computer Aided Energy Management System (EMS)** is a software platform that monitors, controls, and optimises energy use in a facility in real time.

### Key Functions
1. **Real-time monitoring** — Continuously monitors energy consumption of all major loads via sensors and meters.
2. **Data logging and trending** — Records historical data for analysis and reporting.
3. **Alarm management** — Alerts operators when consumption exceeds set limits.
4. **Peak demand control** — Automatically sheds non-critical loads when maximum demand approaches the preset limit.
5. **Load scheduling** — Schedules loads to avoid simultaneous startup of high-demand equipment.
6. **Report generation** — Daily, monthly, annual energy consumption reports; key performance indicators (KPIs).
7. **Predictive analytics** — Uses historical data to forecast energy demand and identify anomalies.

### Advantage when used with SCADA
"EMS and SCADA together" — EMS handles energy optimisation while SCADA handles process control. Integration allows energy-aware process control: e.g., a SCADA alarm for a process deviation automatically triggers EMS to calculate new energy requirements, enabling tighter control and faster response.

## 🟡 5.7 EEM Payback Period Calculation (8 marks — very frequent numerical)

**Standard formula:**

$$\text{Power input} = \frac{\text{Motor rating (kW)} \times \text{\%load}}{100 \times \eta}$$

$$\text{Annual energy} = \text{Power input (kW)} \times \text{Operating hours}$$

$$\text{Annual energy saving} = (\text{Energy}_{old} - \text{Energy}_{new}) \times \text{Cost per kWh}$$

$$\text{Payback period} = \frac{\text{Capital investment}}{\text{Annual saving}}$$

**Numerical (from PYQ — 11 kW motor, 70% load, old η=81%, new η=84.7%, cost Rs 40,000, energy cost Rs 5/kWh):**
- Load on motor = 11 × 0.70 = 7.7 kW
- Input power (old) = 7.7 / 0.81 = 9.506 kW
- Input power (new) = 7.7 / 0.847 = 9.091 kW
- Power saving = 9.506 − 9.091 = 0.415 kW
- Assume 8000 hr/year (use given hours if stated; if not stated, assume 8000)
- Annual energy saving = 0.415 × 8000 = 3320 kWh/year
- Annual cost saving = 3320 × 5 = Rs 16,600
- **Payback period = 40,000 / 16,600 = 2.41 years**
